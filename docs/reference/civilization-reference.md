## Bronze Age Civilizations

### Egyptian

**Time Period**: 3300-1200 BCE (Old Kingdom through New Kingdom)

#### Overview
Ancient Egypt was one of the world's earliest and longest-lasting civilizations, centered on the Nile River. Egyptian civilization was characterized by divine kingship, monumental architecture, and a sophisticated bureaucracy.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: River valleys (Nile dependency)
- **Secondary**: Desert oases
- **Avoid**: Mountains, dense forests
- **Climate**: Hot and arid with fertile river valleys

#### Government Type
- **System**: Divine Monarchy (Theocracy)
- **Ruler Title**: Pharaoh
- **Structure**: Centralized bureaucracy with viziers and nomarchs
- **Characteristics**:
  - Divine right to rule
  - Strong central authority
  - Provincial administration (nomes)
  - Hereditary succession

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Palace Guard, Medjay (desert police)
- **Main Forces**: Spearmen (40%), Archers (30%), Charioteers (20%)
- **Naval**: River patrol boats (10%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Defensive focus
  - Professional standing army
  - Chariot warfare innovation
  - Nubian mercenaries

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: High (80%)
- **Expansionism**: Medium (60%)
- **Fortification**: Medium (70%)
- **Trade Focus**: River-based commerce
- **Writing System**: Hieroglyphics

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Ra, Osiris, Isis, Horus, Anubis, Thoth, Hathor, Set, Ptah

**Deities**:
- **Ra**: Sun god, creator, supreme deity
- **Osiris**: God of the afterlife, resurrection
- **Isis**: Goddess of magic, motherhood
- **Horus**: God of kingship, sky
- **Anubis**: God of mummification, death

**Religious Sites**:
- Pyramids (royal tombs)
- Sun temples
- Valley of the Kings
- Mortuary temples

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Pyramids, Great Sphinx
- **Secondary**: Temples (Karnak, Luxor), Obelisks
- **Infrastructure**: Irrigation canals, granaries
- **Defensive**: Desert forts, river fortifications

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Prefixes: Men-, Theo-, Heli-, Abyd-, Mem-
- Suffixes: -phis, -polis, -otep, -amun, -hotep
- Examples: Memphis, Thebes, Heliopolis, Abydos

**Leaders**:
- Male: Amenhotep, Ramesses, Thutmose, Khufu, Akhenaten
- Female: Hatshepsut, Nefertiti, Cleopatra
- Titles: Pharaoh, Son of Ra, Lord of the Two Lands

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: [Deity]-[Suffix]
- Examples: Ramessid Dynasty, Ptolemaic Dynasty

#### Historical Context
Egyptian civilization developed along the Nile River around 3300 BCE, unifying into a single kingdom under the First Dynasty. The civilization is divided into major periods: Old Kingdom (pyramid builders), Middle Kingdom (classical age), and New Kingdom (imperial expansion). Egyptian culture emphasized the afterlife, cosmic order (ma'at), and the divine nature of kingship.

### Sumerian

**Time Period**: 3300-2000 BCE (Early Dynastic through Ur III)

#### Overview
Sumerians created the world's first urban civilization in Mesopotamia, developing cuneiform writing, the wheel, and the first city-states. Their culture emphasized literacy, law, and religious devotion.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: River valleys (Tigris-Euphrates)
- **Secondary**: Fertile plains
- **Avoid**: Mountains, deserts
- **Climate**: Hot, reliant on irrigation

#### Government Type
- **System**: City-State League
- **Ruler Title**: Lugal (King), Ensi (Governor-Priest)
- **Structure**: Independent cities with shared culture
- **Characteristics**:
  - Multiple competing city-states
  - Theocratic elements (temple authority)
  - Assembly councils
  - Inter-city warfare common

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Royal Guard, Temple Warriors
- **Main Forces**: Spearmen (50%), Militia (30%), Charioteers (15%)
- **Support**: Slingers (5%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Citizen-soldiers
  - Phalanx formations
  - Siege warfare
  - Bronze equipment

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: Very High (90%)
- **Expansionism**: Low (40%)
- **Fortification**: Very High (90%)
- **Trade Focus**: Inter-city commerce
- **Writing System**: Cuneiform

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Anu, Enlil, Enki, Inanna, Utu, Nanna, Ereshkigal

**Deities**:
- **Anu**: Sky god, king of gods
- **Enlil**: God of wind, chief of pantheon
- **Enki**: God of wisdom, water, creation
- **Inanna**: Goddess of love, war, fertility
- **Utu**: Sun god, justice

**Religious Sites**:
- Ziggurats (temple pyramids)
- Temple complexes
- Sacred precincts

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Ziggurats (Ur, Uruk, Eridu)
- **Secondary**: City walls, irrigation systems
- **Infrastructure**: Canals, granaries, archives
- **Defensive**: Fortified city gates, watchtowers

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Forms: Ur, Uruk, Eridu, Lagash, Nippur, Kish
- Pattern: Short, often ending in consonants
- Examples: Ur, Lagash, Nippur, Eridu

**Leaders**:
- Male: Gilgamesh, Ur-Nammu, Sargon, Hammurabi
- Titles: Lugal (King), Ensi (Governor), EN (Lord)

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Named after founding city
- Examples: Dynasty of Ur, Dynasty of Lagash

#### Historical Context
Sumerian civilization emerged in southern Mesopotamia around 3300 BCE, creating the first cities (Uruk, Ur, Eridu). They invented cuneiform writing, the wheel, and organized religion. Sumerian city-states competed for dominance while sharing cultural and religious traditions. Their innovations in writing, mathematics, and law influenced all later Mesopotamian civilizations.

### Minoan

**Time Period**: 2700-1450 BCE (Early through Late Minoan)

#### Overview
The Minoan civilization flourished on Crete and nearby islands, creating a sophisticated maritime culture. Known for elaborate palaces, vibrant frescoes, and peaceful trade networks, Minoans dominated the Aegean Sea.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Islands, coastal regions
- **Secondary**: Harbors, bays
- **Avoid**: Interior continental regions
- **Climate**: Mediterranean, maritime

#### Government Type
- **System**: Palatial Thalassocracy
- **Ruler Title**: Minos (priest-king)
- **Structure**: Palace-centered administration
- **Characteristics**:
  - Theocratic monarchy
  - Peaceful emphasis
  - Trade-focused
  - Minimal military display

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Palace Guard
- **Main Forces**: Light Infantry (30%), Archers (20%)
- **Naval**: War Galleys (40%), Merchant Escorts (10%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Naval superiority
  - Defensive focus
  - Limited standing army
  - Trade protection priority

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: High (85%)
- **Expansionism**: Medium (55%)
- **Fortification**: Low (50%)
- **Trade Focus**: Maritime commerce dominant
- **Writing System**: Linear A

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Mother Goddess, Snake Goddess, Bull God, Maritime Deities

**Deities**:
- **Mother Goddess**: Supreme deity, nature, fertility
- **Snake Goddess**: Protection, household
- **Bull God**: Strength, fertility, ritual
- **Maritime Deities**: Sea, navigation, trade

**Religious Sites**:
- Palace sanctuaries
- Peak sanctuaries (mountain tops)
- Sacred caves
- Bull-leaping arenas

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Palace complexes (Knossos, Phaistos)
- **Secondary**: Harbors, lighthouses
- **Infrastructure**: Roads, aqueducts, drainage systems
- **Defensive**: Coastal watchtowers

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: -ssos, -stos, -thos
- Examples: Knossos, Phaistos, Zakros, Mallia

**Leaders**:
- Male: Minos, Rhadamanthys
- Female: Ariadne, Pasiphae
- Titles: Minos (King), Priestess-Queen

#### Historical Context
Minoan civilization developed on Crete around 2700 BCE, reaching its peak during the Neopalatial period (1700-1450 BCE). The civilization was peaceful, prosperous, and culturally sophisticated. Minoan palaces served as administrative, religious, and economic centers. The civilization ended abruptly around 1450 BCE, possibly due to the Thera eruption and Mycenaean conquest.

### Hittite

**Time Period**: 1650-1200 BCE (Old Kingdom through Late Bronze Age)

#### Overview
The Hittite Empire controlled Anatolia and parts of the Near East, becoming one of the major Bronze Age powers. They were innovators in iron metallurgy and chariot warfare, competing with Egypt and Assyria.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Highlands, mountains
- **Secondary**: Plateaus, defensible terrain
- **Avoid**: Coastal plains, lowlands
- **Climate**: Continental, mountainous

#### Government Type
- **System**: Imperial Monarchy
- **Ruler Title**: Labarna (Great King)
- **Structure**: Feudal-style with vassals
- **Characteristics**:
  - Centralized empire
  - Vassal kingdoms
  - Written treaties
  - Assembly (Pankus) advises king

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Royal Bodyguard, Chariot Corps
- **Main Forces**: Heavy Infantry (35%), Charioteers (30%), Archers (20%)
- **Support**: Slingers (10%), Engineers (5%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Superior chariot tactics
  - Iron weapons (later period)
  - Professional army
  - Mountain warfare expertise

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: Medium (70%)
- **Expansionism**: High (75%)
- **Fortification**: Very High (85%)
- **Trade Focus**: Iron and metallurgy
- **Writing System**: Cuneiform (Hittite), hieroglyphs

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Storm God, Sun Goddess, Telipinu, Ishtar

**Deities**:
- **Storm God** (Teshub): Supreme deity, weather, war
- **Sun Goddess** (Arinna): Queen of gods, state deity
- **Telipinu**: Agriculture, fertility
- **Ishtar**: Love, war (borrowed from Mesopotamia)

**Religious Sites**:
- Rock sanctuaries (Yazılıkaya)
- Temple complexes
- Mountain shrines

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Mountain fortresses, royal archives
- **Secondary**: Rock reliefs, city gates
- **Infrastructure**: Roads, fortified passes
- **Defensive**: Citadels, cyclopean walls

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: -ssa, -tus, -was
- Examples: Hattusa, Alaca, Kanesh, Carchemish

**Leaders**:
- Male: Hattusili, Mursili, Suppiluliuma
- Titles: Labarna (Great King), My Sun

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Hattusili Dynasty, [City]-id Dynasty

#### Historical Context
The Hittite Empire emerged around 1650 BCE in Anatolia, establishing their capital at Hattusa. They became one of the great powers of the Late Bronze Age, competing with Egypt (Battle of Kadesh, 1274 BCE). The Hittites were innovative in iron metallurgy, diplomacy (first peace treaty), and military tactics. The empire collapsed during the Bronze Age Collapse around 1200 BCE.

### Mycenaean

**Time Period**: 1600-1100 BCE (Shaft Grave through Postpalatial)

#### Overview
Mycenaean civilization dominated Bronze Age Greece, building massive fortresses and conducting overseas raids and trade. They were warrior aristocrats, known for their defensive architecture and military prowess.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Hills, defensible heights
- **Secondary**: Fertile plains nearby
- **Avoid**: Flat coastal areas
- **Climate**: Mediterranean

#### Government Type
- **System**: Warrior Kingdoms
- **Ruler Title**: Wanax (King), Lawagetas (War Leader)
- **Structure**: Palace-based monarchies
- **Characteristics**:
  - Warrior aristocracy
  - Regional kingdoms
  - Palace bureaucracy
  - Linear B records

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Royal Guard, Chariot Warriors
- **Main Forces**: Heavy Infantry (40%), Charioteers (25%), Light Infantry (20%)
- **Naval**: War Galleys (10%), Raiders (5%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Bronze armor and weapons
  - Chariot nobility
  - Raiding culture
  - Defensive tactics

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: High (75%)
- **Expansionism**: Medium (65%)
- **Fortification**: Very High (95%)
- **Trade Focus**: Raiding and commerce
- **Writing System**: Linear B

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Early Greek gods (Zeus, Hera, Poseidon, Dionysus, Artemis)

**Deities**:
- **Zeus**: Sky god, chief deity
- **Hera**: Queen of gods, marriage
- **Poseidon**: Earth-shaker, horses, sea
- **Dionysus**: Wine, ecstasy, vegetation
- **Artemis**: Wilderness, hunting

**Religious Sites**:
- Tholos tombs (royal burial)
- Palace sanctuaries
- Sacred springs

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Cyclopean walls (Mycenae, Tiryns)
- **Secondary**: Lion Gate, tholos tombs
- **Infrastructure**: Drainage systems, roads
- **Defensive**: Citadels, fortified acropolis

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: Classical Greek origins
- Examples: Mycenae, Pylos, Tiryns, Thebes

**Leaders**:
- Male: Agamemnon, Nestor, Menelaus
- Titles: Wanax (King), Lawagetas (War Leader)

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Named after legendary founders
- Examples: Atreid Dynasty, Neleid Dynasty

#### Historical Context
Mycenaean civilization arose in mainland Greece around 1600 BCE, eventually conquering Minoan Crete. They built massive fortified palaces at Mycenae, Pylos, and Tiryns. Mycenaeans were warriors and traders, conducting raids throughout the Mediterranean (possibly including the Trojan War). The civilization collapsed during the Bronze Age Collapse (1200-1100 BCE), leading to the Greek Dark Ages.

## Classical Age Civilizations

### Greek

**Time Period**: 800 BCE - 500 CE (Archaic, Classical, Hellenistic periods)

#### Overview
Ancient Greece was not a unified state but a collection of independent city-states (poleis) sharing language and culture. Greeks pioneered democracy, philosophy, theater, and made lasting contributions to Western civilization.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Coastal regions, islands
- **Secondary**: Hills, mountainous terrain
- **Avoid**: Large plains
- **Climate**: Mediterranean

#### Government Type
- **System**: City-State (Polis)
- **Ruler Title**: Varies (Archon, Strategos, Tyrant, King)
- **Structure**: Independent cities with own governments
- **Characteristics**:
  - Democracy (Athens), Oligarchy (Sparta)
  - Citizen assemblies
  - Elected magistrates
  - Independent but culturally unified

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Spartans, Cavalry, Sacred Band
- **Main Forces**: Hoplites (50%), Light Infantry (20%), Peltasts (15%)
- **Naval**: Triremes (15%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Citizen-soldiers
  - Phalanx formation
  - Naval power (Athens)
  - Spartan military culture

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: Very High (85%)
- **Expansionism**: Low (35%)
- **Fortification**: High (80%)
- **Trade Focus**: Maritime commerce, colonization
- **Writing System**: Greek alphabet

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Zeus, Athena, Apollo, Poseidon, Hera, Artemis, Ares, Aphrodite, Hermes, Hephaestus, Dionysus, Demeter

**Deities**:
- **Zeus**: King of gods, sky, thunder
- **Athena**: Wisdom, warfare, patron of Athens
- **Apollo**: Sun, prophecy, arts, Delphi
- **Poseidon**: Sea, earthquakes, horses
- **Artemis**: Hunt, wilderness, moon

**Religious Sites**:
- Temples (Parthenon, Olympia)
- Oracles (Delphi, Dodona)
- Theaters (religious festivals)
- Sacred groves

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Temples, theaters, agora
- **Secondary**: Acropolis, gymnasiums, stadiums
- **Infrastructure**: Roads, harbors, aqueducts
- **Defensive**: City walls, coastal fortifications

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: -opolis, -ia, -os, -us
- Examples: Athens, Sparta, Corinth, Thebes, Marathon

**Leaders**:
- Male: Pericles, Alexander, Leonidas, Themistocles
- Female: Aspasia, Gorgo
- Titles: Archon, Strategos, Basileus (King)

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Macedonian, Seleucid, Ptolemaic
- Examples: Argead Dynasty (Macedonia), Antigonid Dynasty

#### Historical Context
Classical Greek civilization flourished from 800 BCE through the Hellenistic period. Major poleis included Athens (democracy, culture), Sparta (military), Corinth (trade), and Thebes (military power). Greeks fought off Persian invasions (Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis), engaged in the Peloponnesian War, and spread culture through Alexander's conquests. Greek philosophy, drama, democracy, and science influenced all subsequent Western civilization.

### Roman

**Time Period**: 509 BCE - 476 CE (Republic and Empire)

#### Overview
Rome transformed from a city-state to a vast empire controlling the Mediterranean world. Known for military prowess, engineering, law, and administration, Rome created a lasting cultural and political legacy.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Mediterranean climates
- **Secondary**: Varied (empire spans multiple regions)
- **Avoid**: None (adapt to all terrains)
- **Climate**: Mediterranean core, diverse provinces

#### Government Type
- **System**: Republic (early), Empire (later)
- **Ruler Title**: Consul, Dictator, Caesar, Augustus
- **Structure**: Complex constitutional system
- **Characteristics**:
  - Senate and assemblies (Republic)
  - Imperial bureaucracy (Empire)
  - Provincial governors
  - Legal codification

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Praetorian Guard, Legions
- **Main Forces**: Legionnaires (60%), Auxiliaries (25%)
- **Support**: Engineers (8%), Cavalry (7%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Professional standing army
  - Engineering corps
  - Provincial recruitment
  - Naval power

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: Very High (90%)
- **Expansionism**: Very High (95%)
- **Fortification**: High (80%)
- **Trade Focus**: Empire-wide commerce
- **Writing System**: Latin alphabet

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Jupiter, Mars, Venus, Neptune, Juno, Minerva, Apollo, Diana

**Deities**:
- **Jupiter**: King of gods, sky, state protector
- **Mars**: War, father of Romans
- **Venus**: Love, beauty, ancestor of Julian clan
- **Neptune**: Sea, earthquakes
- **Minerva**: Wisdom, crafts, warfare

**Religious Sites**:
- Temples (Pantheon, Temple of Jupiter)
- Forums
- Amphitheaters (religious games)
- Victory monuments

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Roads, aqueducts, forums
- **Secondary**: Amphitheaters, bathhouses, triumphal arches
- **Infrastructure**: Bridges, walls, ports
- **Defensive**: Limes (frontier walls), castrum (forts)

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: -um, -ia, -polis (Greek influence)
- Examples: Rome, Pompeii, Ostia, Mediolanum

**Leaders**:
- Male: Caesar, Augustus, Marcus Aurelius, Trajan
- Female: Livia, Agrippina, Julia
- Titles: Consul, Caesar, Augustus, Imperator

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Named after founder or clan
- Examples: Julio-Claudian, Flavian, Antonine, Severan

#### Historical Context
Rome began as a city-state in 753 BCE, becoming a republic in 509 BCE. After centuries of expansion, the Republic transformed into an Empire under Augustus (27 BCE). At its height (117 CE), Rome controlled the entire Mediterranean, western Europe, Britain, and parts of the Middle East. Roman law, engineering, military tactics, and Latin language influenced European civilization for millennia. The Western Empire fell in 476 CE.

### Persian

**Time Period**: 550 BCE - 651 CE (Achaemenid, Parthian, Sassanian)

#### Overview
The Persian Empire was one of the largest empires in ancient history, stretching from Egypt to India. Persians were known for their tolerance, administrative efficiency, road systems, and Zoroastrian religion.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Highlands, plateaus
- **Secondary**: River valleys, oases
- **Avoid**: None (diverse empire)
- **Climate**: Continental, arid

#### Government Type
- **System**: Imperial Satrapies
- **Ruler Title**: Shahanshah (King of Kings)
- **Structure**: Provincial system with satrapies
- **Characteristics**:
  - Centralized monarchy
  - Provincial governors (satraps)
  - Royal road system
  - Tolerant administration

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Immortals (10,000 elite guard)
- **Main Forces**: Infantry (30%), Cavalry (30%), Archers (25%)
- **Support**: War Elephants (10%), Chariots (5%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Diverse ethnic units
  - Cavalry emphasis
  - Elite Immortals
  - Siege capabilities

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: High (75%)
- **Expansionism**: Very High (85%)
- **Fortification**: High (75%)
- **Trade Focus**: Royal roads, silk road
- **Writing System**: Cuneiform (Old Persian), Pahlavi

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Ahura Mazda, Ahriman, Mithra, Anahita

**Deities**:
- **Ahura Mazda**: Supreme god, wisdom, light
- **Ahriman**: Evil spirit, darkness, chaos
- **Mithra**: Sun, contracts, warriors
- **Anahita**: Water, fertility, warfare

**Religious Sites**:
- Fire temples
- Rock tombs (Naqsh-e Rustam)
- Palaces with religious chambers
- Magi sanctuaries

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Persepolis, royal palaces
- **Secondary**: Fire temples, royal tombs
- **Infrastructure**: Royal roads, qanats (underground canals)
- **Defensive**: Mountain fortresses, desert forts

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: -polis (Greek), -abad, -shahr
- Examples: Persepolis, Susa, Ecbatana, Ctesiphon

**Leaders**:
- Male: Cyrus, Darius, Xerxes, Artaxerxes
- Titles: Shahanshah (King of Kings), Great King

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Achaemenid, Parthian, Sassanian
- Examples: Achaemenid Dynasty, Arsacid Dynasty

#### Historical Context
The Persian Empire was founded by Cyrus the Great (550 BCE), unifying the Medes and Persians. The Achaemenid Empire (550-330 BCE) controlled the largest empire of its time, from Egypt to India. Known for tolerance and efficient administration, Persians allowed conquered peoples religious and cultural freedom. The empire fell to Alexander the Great (330 BCE) but Persian culture continued through Parthian and Sassanian dynasties until the Islamic conquest (651 CE).

### Carthaginian

**Time Period**: 814-146 BCE (Phoenician to Punic Wars)

#### Overview
Carthage was a maritime empire controlling trade in the western Mediterranean. Founded by Phoenicians, Carthaginians were merchant-warriors competing with Rome for Mediterranean dominance.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Coastal regions, harbors
- **Secondary**: Islands, maritime routes
- **Avoid**: Interior continental regions
- **Climate**: Mediterranean

#### Government Type
- **System**: Merchant Republic
- **Ruler Title**: Suffete (Judge), General
- **Structure**: Oligarchic republic
- **Characteristics**:
  - Merchant aristocracy
  - Elected magistrates
  - Council of Elders
  - Professional military

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Sacred Band, Generals' Bodyguard
- **Main Forces**: Mercenaries (50%), Citizen Militia (20%)
- **Naval**: War Galleys (20%), Marines (10%)
- **Special**: War Elephants (elite units)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Naval superiority
  - Mercenary armies
  - War elephants
  - Defensive fortifications

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: Very High (90%)
- **Expansionism**: High (70%)
- **Fortification**: Very High (85%)
- **Trade Focus**: Maritime commerce dominant
- **Writing System**: Phoenician alphabet

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Baal Hammon, Tanit, Melqart, Eshmun, Astarte

**Deities**:
- **Baal Hammon**: Chief god, sky, fertility
- **Tanit**: Mother goddess, chief female deity
- **Melqart**: Protector of city, maritime commerce
- **Eshmun**: Healing, vegetation
- **Astarte**: Love, war, fertility

**Religious Sites**:
- Tophet (ritual sites)
- Temples to Tanit and Baal
- Harbor shrines

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Cothon (circular harbor), city walls
- **Secondary**: Temples, trade ports
- **Infrastructure**: Naval dockyards, lighthouses
- **Defensive**: Triple walls, naval fortifications

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: Phoenician origins, -rt suffix
- Examples: Carthage, Utica, Leptis, Gades

**Leaders**:
- Male: Hannibal, Hamilcar, Hasdrubal
- Female: Dido (legendary founder)
- Titles: Suffete, General, Admiral

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Barcid (Hannibal's family), Magonid

#### Historical Context
Carthage was founded by Phoenicians around 814 BCE, growing into a major maritime power. By 300 BCE, Carthage controlled western Mediterranean trade, competing with Greek colonies and Rome. The Punic Wars (264-146 BCE) pitted Carthage against Rome. Despite Hannibal's brilliant campaigns (crossing the Alps, defeating Rome at Cannae), Carthage ultimately lost. The city was destroyed by Rome in 146 BCE, ending Punic civilization.

### Celtic

**Time Period**: 800 BCE - 100 CE (Iron Age)

#### Overview
Celtic peoples inhabited central and western Europe, organized into tribal groups. Known for warrior culture, druids, and artistic metalwork, Celts competed with Romans and Germanic tribes.

#### Geographic Preferences
- **Primary**: Forests, hills
- **Secondary**: River valleys
- **Avoid**: Arid regions, extreme terrain
- **Climate**: Temperate, forested

#### Government Type
- **System**: Tribal Confederation
- **Ruler Title**: Chief, King, Vergobretus
- **Structure**: Decentralized tribes with assemblies
- **Characteristics**:
  - Tribal councils
  - Warrior aristocracy
  - Druidic priesthood
  - Loose confederations

#### Military Composition
- **Elite Units**: Warrior Bands, Champions
- **Main Forces**: Warriors (40%), Light Infantry (25%), Cavalry (20%)
- **Support**: Chariots (10%), Skirmishers (5%)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Warrior culture
  - Individual combat
  - Chariot warfare (Britain)
  - Ferocious charges

#### Cultural Traits
- **Urbanization**: Medium (60%)
- **Expansionism**: Medium (55%)
- **Fortification**: High (75%)
- **Trade Focus**: Metalwork, cattle
- **Writing System**: Ogham, Latin (later)

#### Religion
**Pantheon**: Lugh, Brigid, Dagda, Morrigan, Cernunnos, Epona

**Deities**:
- **Lugh**: Sun, skills, multi-talented god
- **Brigid**: Fire, poetry, healing, smithcraft
- **Dagda**: Father god, fertility, wisdom
- **Morrigan**: War, fate, death prophecy
- **Cernunnos**: Nature, animals, wilderness
- **Epona**: Horses, fertility

**Religious Sites**:
- Sacred groves
- Stone circles (Stonehenge)
- Hill sanctuaries
- River shrines

#### Landmarks
- **Primary**: Oppida (hill forts), stone circles
- **Secondary**: Sacred groves, warrior stones
- **Infrastructure**: Hill forts, earthworks
- **Defensive**: Ramparts, ditches, palisades

#### Naming Patterns
**Cities**:
- Pattern: -dunum, -magus, -briga
- Examples: Mediolanum, Noviomagus, Vindobona

**Leaders**:
- Male: Vercingetorix, Brennus, Ambiorix
- Female: Boudicca, Cartimandua
- Titles: Chief, King, Vergobretus (elected magistrate)

**Dynasties**:
- Pattern: Tribal names
- Examples: Arverni, Helvetii, Iceni

#### Historical Context
Celtic culture spread across Europe during the Iron Age (800 BCE - 100 CE), reaching peak influence around 300 BCE. Celts sacked Rome (390 BCE) and raided Delphi (279 BCE). Their decentralized tribal system made them vulnerable to Roman expansion. Julius Caesar conquered Gaul (58-50 BCE), while Celtic Britain resisted until Claudius' invasion (43 CE). Celtic culture survived longest in Ireland, which Rome never conquered, preserving Celtic language, law, and traditions.
